# Part 1: Predicting the future - exploring the COMPAS data

During the Lab, we discussed the COMPAS Risk Assessment tool, an algorithm published by Northpointe (now Equivant), that assigns a Risk Score (1-10) that predicts how likely an individual is to commit another crime (to re-offend or recidivate; low score = low risk, high score = high risk). Northpointe ran their own analyses to validate their algorithm, and they claim that it fairly and accurately predicts recidivism.

We also looked at an investigation into the fairness and accuracy of COMPAS by the independent news outlet ProPublica, who claimed that the algorithm is racially-biased: Black individuals are more often to receive a higher score but not re-offend, whereas White individuals are more likely to be given a low score, but actually re-offend. Its important to note that this refers to the algorithms errors - i.e., the way it makes mistakes is not equal across groups.

In their investigation, ProPublica obtained and analyzed COMPAS data regarding 7,214 individuals in Broward County, Florida. Their dataset and analysis are publicly available online. Well use this dataset to evaluate the claims of both Northpointe and ProPublica. They cant both be right can they?

# --- Compute total time in jail from in and out dates (store in a new column)

              'race',
              'juv_fel_count',

              'juv_other_count',
              'priors_count',
              'c_charge_degree',
              'jailtime',
              'decile_score',
              'score_text',
              'is_recid')]

# --- Rename columns (here, order matters!)
# Notice we're now renaming cols in 'compas' -- 'compas_raw' is no longer used
colnames(compas) = c('sex',
                'age',
                'race',
                'juv_fel',
                'juv_misd',
                'juv_other',
                'priors',
                'crime',
                'jailtime',
                'score',

# --- Rename some factor levels for convenience
compas$race[compas$race == 'African-American'] = 'Black'
compas$race[compas$race == 'Caucasian'] = 'White'
compas$age[compas$age == 'Less than 25'] = 'Under 25'
compas$age[compas$age == 'Greater than 45'] = 'Over 45'

# --- Only keep rows where race is white or black, like ProPublica
compas = compas[compas$race == 'White' | compas$race == 'Black', ]

# --- Remove any rows with NA in any column - Random Forests cannot handle NA
# This only removes ~30-ish rows where 'jailtime' wasn't recorded and is NA
compas = compas[complete.cases(compas), ]

# --- Make column types explicit (mainly factors and numbers)
compas$sex = as.factor(compas$sex)
# NOTE: using factor() with the 'levels' argument specifies the ORDER of levels - handy for plotting
compas$age = factor(compas$age, levels = c('Under 25','25 - 45','Over 45'))
compas$race = as.factor(compas$race)
compas$crime = as.factor(compas$crime)
compas$jailtime = as.numeric(compas$jailtime)

These histograms do not show anything about who actually recidivates. This is important, because its possible that differences in risk scores may be due to differences in baseline rates of recidivism, or what are generally called base rates. In Bayesian statistics, these are called prior probabilities, or just priors.

So, lets plot risk scores by both race and recidivism. We could use a line plot, but I think a heatmap would be appropriate here. A heatmap is a type of plot, usually organized as a 2D grid, where the intensity of color represents data values. They are useful especially when x and y axes are categorical.

# --------- Plot risk scores and recidivism --------- #

# First create a data frame containing the frequency of intent by race

# Part 2: Predicting the future - training a predictive model

Now, the point of the COMPAS risk scores is to predict whether an individual will or will not recidivate. Because we know the ground truth (who actually did and did not recidivate), we can train a predictive model using a supervised algorithm.

Training models to make predictions in R can actually be quite easy and straightforward. As in, one-line-of-code easy. Of course, we might be interested in the actual predictive value of the model (who cares HOW it makes decisions, as long as its correct often enough), but we can also use it to investigate the relationships between outcomes (here, recidivism) and predictors (the rest of the variables). Here, well use Random Forests because they are supervised models that are easy to train and interpret. They can also be remarkably accurate and generalizable; this is bona fide machine learning!

Before we go on, we should get a grip on how Random Forest models work. Forests are made of trees (decision trees) - so lets take a look at a single decision tree grown from this data set. Its unfortunately difficult to visualize one tree with the randomForest package, but we can with tree, which you installed and libraryd above. This tree alone is probably not a good classifier. If it performs slightly better than random guessing, we call it a weak learner. Random Forests are a type of ensemble method, where many weak learners are combined to produce a single prediction. Even if individuals (single Trees) make decisions slightly better than random guessing, as a group they can reach near-perfect accuracy (See here for more). Luckily its very easy to train a Random Forest in R. The basic code to do so is below run the chunk and well inspect the results.

This function will work exactly like other R functions, for example: mean(), that takes arguments (a list of numbers) and returns a value (a single number, the average of the input numbers).

getPredictionError = function(model, dataframe)
{
  # This function assumes the dataframe has columns 'recid' and 'race'
  # and that 'race' has the levels 'Black' and 'White'
  preds = predict(model, dataframe)
  recid = dataframe$recid
  race = dataframe$race
  
  t = table(recid, preds, race)
  
  t[,,'Black'] = t[,,'Black'] / sum(t[,,'Black'])
  t[,,'White'] = t[,,'White'] / sum(t[,,'White'])
  
  t = as.data.frame(t)
  t$type = NA
  for (i in 1:nrow(t))
  {
    if (t[i,'recid'] == 0 & t[i,'preds'] == 0) {
      t[i,'type'] = 'true neg'
    } else if (t[i,'recid'] == 1 & t[i,'preds'] == 1) {
      t[i,'type'] = 'true pos'
    } else if (t[i,'recid'] ==  0 & t[i,'preds'] == 1) {
      t[i,'type'] = 'false pos'
    } else if (t[i,'recid'] ==  1 & t[i,'preds'] == 0) {
      t[i,'type'] = 'false neg'
    }
  }
  t$type = as.factor(t$type)
  
  # Total % errors
  # These lines print to the console for convenience!
  print('Total error for Black defendants:')
  print(sum(t[t$race == 'Black' & grepl('false', t$type), 'Freq']))
  print('Total error for White defendants:')
  print(sum(t[t$race == 'White' & grepl('false', t$type), 'Freq']))
  
  print('Error ratio for Black defendants:')
  print(t[t$race == 'Black' & t$type == 'false pos', 'Freq'] / t[t$race == 'Black' & t$type == 'false neg', 'Freq'])
  print('Error ratio for White defendants:')
  print(t[t$race == 'White' & t$type == 'false pos', 'Freq'] / t[t$race == 'White' & t$type == 'false neg', 'Freq'])
  
  return(t)
}

One criterion for algorithmic fairness that people discuss is called Error ratio parity, meaning that the Error ratios should be the same when comparing groups (i.e.Black vs White, or Male vs Female). If they arent, there is a dis-parity. There are ways to force Error ratio parity on the model, but well get into that another time.

Maybe theres something else we can do to make the algorithm more fair, since we do have some control over what input data go into model training. Another criterion is called Demographic parity or, vaguely, statistical parity, in which an outcome (here, did or did not recidivate) is equally likely across groups, even if base rates in the population actually do differ. We could force Demographic parity onto the model by selecting a sub-sample of the original data in which equal numbers of Black and White defendants do and do not recidivate:

# Told you we'd keep coming back to logical indexing ;)
# Below we're just grabbing a random sample of 1000 row numbers
# for each combination of 'race' and 'recid'
idx1 = sample(which(compas$race == 'Black' & compas$recid == 1), 1000, replace = FALSE)
idx2 = sample(which(compas$race == 'Black' & compas$recid == 0), 1000, replace = FALSE)
idx3 = sample(which(compas$race == 'White' & compas$recid == 1), 1000, replace = FALSE)
idx4 = sample(which(compas$race == 'White' & compas$recid == 0), 1000, replace = FALSE)

# Combine them all at the end
idx = c(idx1, idx2, idx3, idx4)

# If you're curious, take a look at idx - it is not TRUE/FALSE like usual,
# instead it's the actual row numbers that come from which()
# Use idx now to get a subset of 'compas'
compas_parity = compas[idx,]

A common theme in the literature around fairness and bias in predictive algorithms is this: when you adjust the model to be more fair in one direction, you necessarily become less fair AND/OR less accurate in other directions. Here, ensuring Demographic parity in recidivism led to MORE dis-parity in Error ratios - meaning the differences in types of errors were exacerbated!

This is all to say that seeking fair prediction algorithms is challenging and nuanced. This is not a realm of tried-and-true right answers. In cases such as this - we may be suspicious that theres something deeper going on in the underlying data. Ill leave you with an interesting TED talk that describes how a group of researchers did a lot of work on this same dataset using similar methods that you used in this assignment (optional viewing). They concluded that the source of bias may be in the data themselves - Black individuals are policed more heavily, thus are more likely to be arrested, thus their base rates for recidivism are higher than for White individuals. The algorithm itself is not biased of its own accord, but instead is picking up on existing social inequalities.
